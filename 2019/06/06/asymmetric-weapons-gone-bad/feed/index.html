]]> By: forsscmajor https://slatestarcodex.com/2019/06/06/asymmetric-weapons-gone-bad/#comment-763922 Tue, 18 Jun 2019 18:15:16 +0000 https://slatestarcodex.com/?p=5492#comment-763922 You’ve hit upon the exact mistake I made over and over again growing up, and which I suspect to be one of the major handicaps of smart kids who “inexplicably” fail to achieve social success.

I assumed that if someone believed something, they could tell me why they believed it. And I believed that they were good representatives of their position. That is, if they were right about something, I believed they would know some of the better reasons for believing it So, if they could not tell me it was true or useful, or they gave poor reasons for it, I assumed they were wrong.

Small talk, in particular, I remember analyzing this way. “Why should I participate in small talk? It’s boring.” And no one could tell me why, so I didn’t do it, and while it didn’t ruin my life, it did slow my social growth down. Looking back now, and this applies to a lot of things, actually, I would say that people don’t explicitly know most of what they know. They do know it, they know a lot of things, but they don’t explicitly know it; they can’t articulate it. Small talk is a good example, but I think humor is another one, and a very difficult one.

Think about humor for a minute. Can you really explain why something is funny? No? Well, better not laugh, then, right? Comics have come under fire for “edgy” or inappropriate jokes, and some people seem to think there’s not really a problem. You can joke about suffering and death and so on, and it’s not actually a problem, they say. But we can ask them, “Should we really be making light of the most horrendous things to ever plague humanity?” Obviously, it seems like the answer should be “no.”

And yet…I sometimes have this sense myself. I hear a joke and it’s just awful, it absolutely is awful, and I don’t deny the awfulness of it one bit, so please don’t mistake my joyous laughter for a sign that I don’t think the joke is awful and the topic is awful. I’m just laughing anyway. Why? Because I’m an awful person? Well, maybe, or maybe…well, I don’t know.

But maybe we don’t understand humor very well. We can’t explain what’s funny and what’s not, and we can’t say exactly what the effect of dark humor is. At the same time, I don’t really see anything bad happening because of it, and there is a sense in me that dark humor is not hurting us.

What am I supposed to do with a vague sense that something is okay, or that something is wrong? Once upon a time, I would have thrown it out the window. “Only believe in things you understand,” that’s the motto. “Only believe in things you can explain,” might be part of it as well. But I’ll bet that most people are like me in that, if you asked us to justify our belief that Brazil exists, something that seems so basic and obvious, we would actually have to stop and think about how we know this. “Everyone knows that,” ooh, I just know I’m going to get hammered if I try and use a weak argument like that. “I read about it and I’ve seen people talk about it in the news” doesn’t really sound airtight, either. Don’t we know the news is less than perfectly trustworthy, and books as well? I feel pretty confident that Brazil exists, but I don’t know that I can exactly tell you why.

This is the same mistake that the Pharisees made in the New Testament. They knew there was a law against harvesting on the Sabbath, so they saw Jesus and his folks picking some food and eating it, and they said, “Well, you’re picking up the food, right? You are physically taking the fruit off of the plant it’s growing from. That’s the definition of harvesting. Therefore, you are harvesting! Therefore, you are breaking the law!”

Explicitly, this is a hard argument to refute. I’m not denying that I’m picking the fruit from the plant, and if you ask just about anyone, that’s pretty much the exact definition they’ll give for “harvesting.” You can definitely use the non-central example debate here, and it’s probably right, but who’s to say exactly how non-central an example it takes before something switches from “breaking God’s law” to “not breaking God’s law”?

As a result of these ideas, whenever I ask someone to explain or justify something, I listen to them, and I try to keep floating around in the back of my head “this is just what they can articulate right this moment; there may very well be more to all of this.” And if they say “I think there’s something else going on about XYZ, but I can’t quite explain what it is,” then I treat that with a non-trivial amount of respect.

This is very much what I think is going on with ideas like “rights” and also in religion. On the face of it, people just have religious beliefs, and it’s only incidental whether or not they’re happy about them or whatever. After all, I believe all sorts of unhappy facts about the world, maybe the same is true of a lot of religious people with what they have concluded are facts about supernatural entities, right? But in reality, I suspect that there’s almost ZERO overlap between why people really accept/believe in their religion (and in rights) and what they tell you is the “evidence” for their religion (and people are almost always happy about the religious propositions they just so happen to believe are true as well as happy).

There’s a whole long list of “Street Epistemology” videos on YT where they get people to admit within 5 minutes that they basically don’t have any reason to justify their belief in God, and while people will then semi-regularly say that they’re a little less sure that God exists, mostly they change very little, or not at all.

You can extract another example out of political iconography and so on. It’s actually quite hard to justify why having your banners up around the city makes much of a difference, or having a high-quality patriotic song that people can sing. Does breaking the protocol during patriotic displays (like kneeling during the national anthem) really have any larger effect? How? On what? It can be hard to say what good these things are, so we might be tempted to say that they’re just meaningless frills, we can take them or leave them, they don’t really matter. But we know that powerful movements and parties prioritize creating merchandise and mottoes and pledges and songs and rituals and so on. It’s always possible that they’re wrong and that these are worthless endeavors, but, then again, given that these are typical of successful movements, maybe they’re not so worthless after all.

And if we can’t show you what effects they have, and if we can’t point directly at the causal pathway and the mechanism of the effect…maybe that’s not such a knockdown argument after all.

Illegibility is complicated and context-dependent. Fetishes are pretty illegible, but because we have a shared idea of a fetish, because most people have fetishes, and because even the people who don’t have fetishes have the weird-if-you-think-about-it habit of being sexually attracted to other human beings – people can just say “That’s my fetish” and it becomes kind of legible. We don’t question it. And there are all sorts of phrases like “I don’t like it”, or “It’s a free country” or “Because it makes me happy” that sort of relieve us of the difficult work of maintaining legibility for all of our decisions.

Another way to make illegible preferences legible is for them to be part of your religion. If you hate the taste of pork and pork products, you’d probably have an easier time explaining it if you converted to Islam than just trying to convince people that you genuinely hate bacon. Similarly if you decide that for mental health reasons you need one day a week where you don’t do anything, and you want to convey to people that you are absolutely serious about that one day being your rest day, telling people you’re a Jew who observes the Sabbath is going to give you way more credibility than, “Saturday is my day off, no exceptions.” Of course this does limit legibility to established religions, so Scott probably can’t get away with claiming his church doesn’t let him talk on the phone except for emergencies, but nonetheless religion is a traditional way to codify otherwise illegible advice and preferences.
