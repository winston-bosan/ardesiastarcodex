Another topic worth looking at is the social stigma with divorce, which might be greater in some groups vs. others.

To summarise, it was (and to some extent still is) common practice to teach Latin and Ancient Greek using well-known classical texts, which examiners *also* used to set questions. This meant there was a fair chance that any given passage on the exam paper was one that the student had seen before. The passages in the Harvard exam are from famous works by Cicero, Livy, and Aelian, so we should probably account for the chance that some students might have read the original Latin and Greek before taking the exam.

From my own viewpoint: I only did GCSE Latin (GCSEs = UK high school exams, usually taken at age 16 after 2-3 years of study). I certainly wouldn’t have done well at the Harvard exam, but I would have had a rough idea of what I was doing. If I’d carried on studying Latin to university entrance level, I reckon I could’ve managed a respectable mark. I’d probably struggle at least as much to get into modern-day Harvard, honestly.
