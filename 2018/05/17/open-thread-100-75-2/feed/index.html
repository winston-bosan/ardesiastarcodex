]]> By: syrrim https://slatestarcodex.com/2018/05/17/open-thread-100-75-2/#comment-632005 Wed, 23 May 2018 03:29:40 +0000 http://slatestarcodex.com/?p=4913#comment-632005 In reply to Calion.

You can’t own 1/1000 of a lake. That doesn’t make any sense. You could own the land under the lake I suppose, and get mad at people for trespassing. You could own some of the water in the lake, and maybe add a chemical tracer to make sure no one can steal it – but then your water would quickly get diluted with everyone else, and flow out of the ocean pretty quickly anyways. But the lake itself is a concept at best, and not one that allows itself to easy division.

What you could do would be set up a corporation to own the whole lake. Then people could buy shares in the corporation. If you owned shares in the corporation, you would have a say in how the lake as a whole would be treated. This would degenerate in the worst case into a democracy, which is precisely the system we were trying to avoid with this libertarian mumbo-jumbo. Dividing something up into property is supposed to let people do whatever the hell they want with their property. Instead, we have a democracy – where everyone will follow the lead of the most conservative among them, but for rare baby steps of proven progress.

This is an argument for why britain succeeded in the early modern period – they managed to divide their agricultural land into large plots owned by one person (“enclosed field”), whereas everyone else (in europe) had large plots divided into many smaller plots that were rotated between farmers (“open field”). One of the major innovations of that era was crop rotation – basically you plant crops that fixate nitrogen (eg clover) one year, then your real crops will grow better the next year. Open fields were much slower to adopt these systems, because the individual farmer didn’t have much incentive to fixate nitrogen on a field they wouldn’t be farming the next year – open fields only adopted this system after achieving rough consensus among the farmers.

Basically, libertarianism says: “what if we made everything an enclosed field?” – but you can’t enclose a lake.
