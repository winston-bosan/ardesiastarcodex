]]> By: Harry Maurice Johnston https://slatestarcodex.com/2018/01/02/2017-predictions-calibration-results/#comment-585399 Fri, 05 Jan 2018 23:04:46 +0000 http://slatestarcodex.com/?p=4735#comment-585399 In reply to Harry Maurice Johnston.

Sorry, too counter-intuitive for me. The daily estimates are being made at different times, after all, and the way you round the probabilities (to the nearest 1%, 5%, 10%?) would affect which ones become part of the “same prediction”.

(It also seems wrong that you don’t know whether a particular daily prediction will be part of the “same prediction” as another one until after you’ve made them, though I’m less sure about that.)

But even if we use your language, we still need to understand why it seems sensible to categorize the weather report for days 1, 3, 4, 9 and 15 as a single prediction but does not seem sensible to categorize someone’s answers to questions 1, 3, 4, 9 and 15 as a single prediction.

I now think it has to do with the canonical form of the questions; there’s an obvious relationship between “will it rain on the 5th” and “will it rain on the 7th”.

But to skip to a different example, imagine someone who is just generally overoptimistic and made predictions like this for 2017:

* Israel and Palestine will sign a formal peace treaty, 50%

* I’m going to go to the gym every day, 50%

* The EMDrive will be proven to work, 50%

* No new reality shows on TV, 50%

… and so on, noting that it is perfectly natural for an optimistic person to consistently phrase every prediction as “what they want to happen, 50%” rather than “what they don’t want to happen, 50%”. [Can’t take credit for this idea, someone mentioned it in another sub-thread.]

There’s still a canonical form, but it’s entirely inside the person’s head, and I can’t help but think that the differences between that person’s estimates and the estimates a more detached person might make is also relevant.

I should add that I understand and agree that it is possible for badly-calibrated 50% estimates to nonetheless look well-calibrated when analyzed in this way. But it doesn’t have to happen, and I’m still not convinced that either of us properly understand under what circumstances it should or should not be expected.
